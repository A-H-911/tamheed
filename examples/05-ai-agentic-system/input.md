# Project Brief — support-triage-agent (input as provided)

Our customer-support team is drowning. Inbound email volume has roughly tripled over two years and headcount has not kept pace. Agents spend most of their day on repetitive triage — reading each email, figuring out what it's about and how urgent it is, digging through our knowledge base for the right answer, and typing a reply. A large share of these are variations on the same handful of questions. The team is burning out, first-response times are sliding, and we're missing our SLAs on the genuinely urgent tickets because they're buried in the noise.

I want us to build an AI agent that triages inbound support email and drafts replies, so humans spend their time reviewing and sending rather than starting from a blank page. The pipeline I have in mind: ingest each incoming email, classify it into a category and an urgency level, retrieve the relevant knowledge-base context for it, draft a proposed reply grounded in that context, and then either route/escalate the email to the right human queue or present the draft to a human for approval. The agent assists; humans stay in control.

This is a customer-facing system, so safety is paramount and these are non-negotiable. (1) A human must approve every reply before it is sent — no exceptions at launch. (2) The agent must never make up facts. Every claim in a draft has to be grounded in retrieved knowledge-base content; if it can't ground an answer, it defers to a human rather than guessing. (3) It must protect customer PII — no leaking personal data to tools, models, or logs that shouldn't see it. (4) It must stay within a bounded budget — a fixed tool allow-list and a hard cap on iterations and cost per email, so we never get a runaway loop quietly burning money. (5) Everything it does — every tool call, every decision, with rationale — must be logged so we have a full audit trail.

Cross-team context: Support Operations owns the category and urgency taxonomy and the escalation rules, and they approve how triage behaves. Security owns the PII handling and retention policy and what counts as sensitive data. Engineering builds and operates the system. I do not want us hard-wired to a single model vendor — abstract the model provider, the email system, the knowledge base, and the human-review queue behind clean boundaries so we can swap any of them.

Success, in business terms: a deflection / auto-handle rate that meaningfully reduces the queue, measurable human handling-time saved per ticket, and reply quality/accuracy that does not regress versus humans. I would love to eventually let the agent auto-send replies for a small set of safe, low-risk categories — but only once we have measured its accuracy and earned trust. That is a later phase, not day one, and I expect it to be gated on real evidence.

Open questions I do not have firm answers to, and I'd rather you surface them than guess. What classification accuracy bar should we hold the agent to? What end-to-end latency per email is acceptable? Which categories, if any, could ever be safe to auto-send? What exactly counts as PII and what's the handling policy? Do we have a labeled corpus of past emails to evaluate against, and how big? And what's the per-email cost budget? Treat these as things to pin down, not to assume silently.

The outcome I want: a support-triage agent the team trusts, that cuts handling time without ever risking a wrong, embarrassing, or unsafe reply to a customer, that we can defend in an audit — and a plan I can hand to engineering to execute.
