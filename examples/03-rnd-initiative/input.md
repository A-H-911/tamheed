# Project Brief — semantic-cache (input as provided)

We spend a lot of money on our LLM API, and a meaningful chunk of our latency budget goes to waiting on it. When we look at our traffic, a striking amount of it is near-duplicate: people ask the same thing in slightly different words, the same FAQ-style questions recur constantly, and within a session users rephrase and retry. We are paying full price and full latency for answers we have, in effect, already produced. The idea we want investigated is a **semantic cache** that sits in front of the LLM API. Instead of caching on an exact-string key (which almost never hits for free-text prompts), we embed the incoming prompt, look for a stored prompt that is semantically similar above some threshold, and — if we find one — return its cached answer instead of calling the model. The promise is obvious: lower cost, lower latency, on a big slice of traffic, for relatively little infrastructure.

The worry is just as obvious, and it is the thing that keeps us up at night. A semantic cache can be **confidently wrong**. If the similarity threshold is too loose, we will serve a cached answer to a prompt that *looks* similar but actually needs a different response — and the user gets a fluent, plausible, wrong answer with no indication anything went sideways. A cache *miss* is cheap and safe (we just call the model). A *false positive* — a wrong-context or stale answer served as if correct — is genuinely worse than not having a cache at all, because it silently degrades answer quality and trust. So this is not a "build a cache" task. It is a "find out whether a cache is even a good idea here, and under what settings" task.

Honestly, we do not know the answers to the core questions, and we are not going to pretend we do:

- On **our** workload, what hit-rate can we actually achieve, and at what false-positive rate? That trade-off is the whole game and we have no data on it yet.
- Which **embedding approach** gives us the cleanest separation between "genuinely the same question" and "looks similar but isn't" — and at what latency and cost? A heavier embedding model might separate better but could eat the latency win.
- Where does the **similarity threshold** actually need to sit, and how stable is that across question types?
- How do we keep cached answers **fresh**? Some answers go stale (facts change, underlying data updates). What invalidation strategy keeps staleness-related wrong answers low?
- How do we handle **personalized or context-dependent** prompts, where the "right" answer depends on who is asking or on session state? Those must never be cross-served between users.

We would rather state success as a **hypothesis to test** than as a feature to ship. Something like: *we believe we can cut LLM cost by at least some target percentage on our representative workload while holding the false-positive (wrong-answer) rate at or below an acceptable bar.* If that holds, great — we productionize. If it does not hold on our traffic, that is a perfectly good result too, and we want it documented clearly so we stop spending on this.

Constraints we already know: answer quality must not drop below an acceptable bar (a wrong cached answer counts against us). And the cached prompts and responses are sensitive — we have privacy and retention obligations on storing user prompts and model outputs, and nothing should leak across users.

To be explicit about framing: **Phase 1 of this is to LEARN, not to ship.** The deliverable of the first phase is *knowledge* — a set of validated or refuted hypotheses, a characterized cost/false-positive trade-off, and a clear **go/no-go recommendation on productionization** — not a finished caching product. We would rather you ask us the sharp questions than assume. The things we have not pinned down: the exact cost-reduction target and the maximum acceptable false-positive rate; what the representative evaluation workload/dataset is and how we judge whether an answer is "correct"; the latency budget for a cache lookup; the privacy/retention rules for cached data; and what "wrong answer" even means operationally so we can measure it.
